<h3>Thomas Hope</h3>
<p>
Thomas Hope (1770-1831) was born in Amsterdam into a wealthy family of Scottish descent who, as one of the richest merchant bankers of Europe, frequently gave loans to noble dynasties and governments. When he was eighteen years old, Hope, similar to many of his age and class, embarked on a Grand Tour. During the first leg, beginning in 1787, he visited Germany, Italy, Spain, and North Africa, then the second leg in 1796-97 took him to Greece, Egypt, and Turkey (all then under Ottoman control). Hope’s family was forced to leave Amsterdam for London following the French invasion in 1794. In the following years, likely inspired by English sculpture galleries, Hope busied himself with collecting Italian sculptures for display in his estates, as well as Southern Italian Greek pottery. By 1806, Hope owned more than a thousand and five hundred vases, some of which came from the collection of Sir William Hamilton, the British envoy to the Kingdom of Naples.<br><br>
The places Hope visited during his Tour had a profound impact on him: he filled five volumes with drawings from the second leg, and the sights inspired his endeavours into architectural and interior design. Hope not only wished to display the items he owned, but to also immerse visitors to his Duchess Street home in the world of the ancients. One of Hope’s beliefs was that vases were mainly intended for the dead, which led him to describe the backdrop of his collection as an environment of “mystic death and regeneration.” As such, his enormous collection of vases were housed in four rooms modelled after Roman columbaria, public structures built for the storage of cinerary urns.<br><br>
In 1807, following his marriage to Louisa de la Poer Beresford, the daughter of the first Baron of Decies, Hope acquired The Deepdene in Surrey, where he sought to reimagine the concept of the modern country house, remodelling the red-brick Georgian mansion and extending his vision beyond interiors and urban space into the pastoral landscape.  <br><br>
- Kata Gelencser</p>

<h3>What was the Grand Tour?</h3>
<p>Grand Tours were the name given to the journey people embarked on, typically from the seventeenth through to the nineteenth century. Sons of extremely wealthy families, usually from Britain but also other countries such as the Netherlands. Grand Tours served many different purposes. People could send their sons on these tours to make them better gentlemen through establishing connections with well-to-do people or for purely educational purposes.<br><br>
The thought behind sending their sons on a Grand Tour of their own is that they wanted to broaden their minds and educate them more about the world through interacting with other cultures. While abroad they would interact with the biggest historical attractions and were instructed to take notes on everything they saw while travelling, from architecture to artifacts. It was common for these tourists to take artefacts of things they like without a second thought and it was actually expected of them to do so. The items they would take could be as small as a little ceramic cup or as big as a statue, if their entourage could carry it.<br><br> 
Typically, the traveller would go across most of Europe, to places such as France, Switzerland, and Italy. It would depend on the family who was funding the trip whether they would send their son to places further away such as Greece or beyond. It would also be more dangerous to send their sons further away due having to travel over more seas or over mountains, or if the country they were sent to were involved in violent conflicts.This makes Thomas Hope’s Grand Tour even more exceptional as he went to places in Africa and Asia, which was quite uncommon. <br><br>
- Ethan Anderson</p>

<h3>Hope's Interior Design</h3>
<p>Thomas Hope played an influential part in early eighteenth century Regency era design. A collector, designer, and tastemaker, Hope played a central role in defining the Regency Style (c. 1800-1830), a period marked by refined elegance and a revival of classical antiquity. Deeply shaped by his extensive travels on the Grand Tour, Hope drew inspiration from the art and architecture of ancient civilizations, translating these influences into interiors, furniture, and decorative objects that were both scholarly and innovative. Hope’s distinctive approach, often described as Regency Classicism, combined forms and motifs derived from ancient Greece and Rome with stylistic elements drawn from ancient Egypt and the French Rococo tradition of the mid-eighteenth century. This eclectic synthesis resulted in interiors that were both historically informed and strikingly modern, blending austere classical geometry with ornamental richness and symbolic depth.<br><br>
Working in association with the prominent architect Robert Adam, Hope created a series of highly curated interiors in The Duchess House that functioned as immersive environments rather than mere living spaces. The house quickly became a cultural landmark, attracting visitors from across Britain and beyond. In 1802, Hope famously opened the house with a grand reception attended by the Prince of Wales, George Augustus Frederick, underscoring its importance within elite social and artistic circles.<br><br>
<img src="HopeRoomColour.jpg" alt="Hope Room Art1" style="width:128px;height:128px;"> 
<img src="HopeRoomSketch.jpg" alt="Hope Room Art2" style="width:128px;height:128px;"> 
Hope’s Duchess Street house featured a sequence of themed rooms, each reflecting a different historical or aesthetic inspiration. The Egyptian Room demonstrated his belief in the foundational role of ancient Egyptian culture in shaping Western artistic traditions. The Statue Gallery displayed an array of white marble sculptures, evoking the ideals of classical antiquity, while the Aurora Room centered on a statue of Aurora, the Roman goddess of dawn. Beginning in 1804, Hope began charging admission to members of the Royal Academy, effectively transforming his home into one of the earliest public exhibitions of interior design.<br><br>
In 1807, Hope published Household Furniture and Interior Decoration Executed from Designs by Thomas Hope, a richly illustrated volume that documented his work at Duchess Street. This publication proved immensely influential, widely regarded as one of the first books to formalize the discipline of interior decoration. It became an essential reference for designers throughout the nineteenth century, disseminating Hope’s aesthetic principles far beyond his own projects.<br><br>
<img src="HopeChair.jpg" alt="Hope Chair" style="width:128px;height:128px;"> 
<img src="HopeChaise.jpg" alt="Hope Chaise" style="width:128px;height:128px;"> 
That same year, Hope acquired The Deepdene, where he sought to reimagine the concept of the modern country house. The existing red-brick Georgian mansion was extensively remodeled, allowing Hope to continue experimenting with architectural form and interior arrangement, extending his vision beyond urban space into the pastoral landscape.<br><br>
After Hope’s death in 1831, his influence persisted through publications, trade journals, and the continued circulation of his designs. Although the Duchess Street house was demolished in 1851, its contents were transferred to The Deepdene, where they remained accessible to the public. The dispersal of Hopes collection in 1917 played a significant role in reviving interest in his work, contributing to the Regency Revival of the 1920s and 1930s and even leaving traces in the emerging aesthetic of Art Deco.<br><br>
Today, Thomas Hope is remembered not only as a designer but as a visionary who helped establish interior decoration as both an art form and a discipline. His work bridged past and present, transforming historical knowledge into immersive environments that continue to shape our understanding of design.<br><br>
- Brianna Ajamian</p>

<h3>Hamilton Contributions</h3>
<p>By the end of his life in, Thomas Hope had come to amass a considerable collection of Greek and Etruscan vases. It was the cumulation of a long career of collecting that had started in Rome, all the way back in 1795. But our ability to understand how he’d achieved this, or where such artefacts had come from are limited. For after his death, Hope’s sons had had little interest in keeping their father’s papers. This then raises the question- what do we know about Thomas Hope and his collections? Firstly, there are a number of works associated with the man himself; be it directly or indirectly. Of these, the engravings of Tischbein offer valuable insight into the contents of what later became parts of Hope’s collection. Other insights can also be gleamed from Hope’s correspondences with his dilettante colleagues- such as a Sir William Hamilton.<br><br>
Unlike Hope, the correspondences of Sir William Hamilton are more intact. By 1796, his own collection had numbered about 1000. Yet this impressive achievement would not grow much larger, 
however. He’d much feared the then approaching Napoleon. Of this situation, he’d written: <q>“I think that Italy is in is in great danger of being completely plunder'd and ruin'd”.</q> So close 
indeed had the French been that a commissary of theirs had been lodged in Hamilton’s own Neapolitan home. It is also clear, that Hamilton’s concerns were not unfounded, for he claims that 
they had <q>“made use of my furniture... went off with three of my carriages."</q> Moreover, Hamilton also believed that a similar fate would have befallen the other parts of his collection had he 
not acted. Unnerved by the prospect of further losses, Hamilton had turned to one of his many ‘friends in high places’. This friend was one ‘Lord Nelson’, better known to the rest of us as
Horatio Nelson. Of this arrangement, Hamilton wrote: <q>“...by [ Lord Nelson’s] friendship I have at least secured all my best pictures and collection of vases on board of one of his 
victualling transports.”</q><br><br>
This ‘victualling transport’, was the HMS Colossus. A third-rate ship of the line, it boasted a complement of 74 cannons. Indeed, Hamilton’s assurances of being safe on a ‘good transport’ 
were no exaggeration. However, even such a formidable vessel was beholden to the whims of the sea and its weather. While waiting to cross from the Isles of Scilly, an unexpected gale had 
caused an anchor cable to snap. This seemingly minor incident snowballed, causing the warship’s eventual sinking by the 11 December 1798. With it, also went one-third of the ‘cream’ of 
Hamilton’s Second Collection.  Hamilton had been devastated, writing that: <q>“As to my vases that is a sore point, they had better be at Paris than at the bottom of the sea...I can’t bear to
look at some remaining cases here in which I know there are only black vases without figures.”</q> Despite this, Hamilton had remained hopeful. In the same letter, he commented how <q>“they were 
excellently packed up & the cases will not easily go to pieces, & the sea water will not hurt the vases”.</q> Thankfully, it was realised that the lost third had represented ‘some of the worst’
, rather than the ‘cream’ as once assumed.<br><br>
But what became of the two-thirds that had survived? Sold. After returning to England for good in 1800, Hamilton had decided to part with most of his collections. Initially, this was meant
to be done through the services of Mr Christie- the founder of the auction house of the same name. Three of these sales ultimately happened.<br><br>
he’d arranged to have his Second Collection- the ‘cream of the crop’ shipped to England. Its escort too, left little more to be desired; a well-armed, third-rate ship of the line- the HMS
Colossus. However not even such a ship would be exempt from the whims of the sea and its weather. Hamilton would lose-by his own estimation- a third of his then-burgeoning Second Collection
. But what of the remaining 2/3? Sold. In 1801, these survivors found themselves on sale at Christie’s. Finally, they would wind up in the possession of Thomas Hope for 4500 guineas- or 
about £300,000 today. Hamilton had written to his friend, C.F. Greville on April 3rd, 1801, about this sale- which reveal much about his motivations:<br><br>
<q>“Christie to inform him, but the advertisement was gone. He will publish another that the collection has been disposed of by private contract, by which means they will not be dispersed, 
but remain together for the inspection of the learned antiquaries and artists”.</q><br><br>
It was for this reason then, that Hamilton had sold the remnants of his Second Collection to one person; and why he’d accepted it when Hope had underbid for it:<br><br>
This morning Mr. Thos. Hope came to me, and having offered the round sum of four thousand pound down for my whole collection of vases for which I had asked £5000.<br><br>
It was also this purchase that would make Hope’s own name and reputation as a collector. Indeed, according to the estimates of A.L Millin- a good friend of Hope’s- that his collection had risen to some 1500 vases. Yet even still, Hope’s career remained a flurry of activity; both buying and selling classical antiquities. The former had primarily came from a series of sales; at Cawdor, Chinnery, Edwards, Coghill, de Paroi, Durand, Magnoncourt and Beugnot.  As for the latter, Hope had sold some 180 pieces of his collection in 1805 alone. However, even after Hope’s death on February 2nd 1831- a significant portion of his collections remained. It were these remnants that were sold off for good in 1917.<br><br>
- Benedek Fenyvesi</p>

<h3>How Did They End Up Here?</h3>
<p>Shortly after Thomas’ death in 1831, his son Henry sold parts of the vase collection and moved the remaining pieces to another family property; Deepdene in Surrey - as well as having the Duchess Street residence demolished. In 1917 Thomas’ grandson Francis sold much of the collection’s remnants, and on the 23rd of July of the same year Christie's Auction House conducted the Hope Heirloom sale, which resulted in the distribution of the collection across Britain and America - landing in museums and private collections. Some of these artefacts were acquired for the Irish National collection, despite the difficulties presented by the turbulence of that time period. This effort was assisted by sponsorships from various prominent figures in Ireland for the Classical Museum of UCD. <br><br>
The Classical Museum now displays our own collection of vases from this collection, as well as two on loan from the National Museum of Ireland (NMI 1917.41 & NMI 1917.52), totalling 24 vases on site. <br><br>
<b>Sponsors</b><br><br>
Noted sponsors of the acquisition of our collection include the Rt. Hon. Lawrence Waldron, Esq., Mr. Alfred Smith Esq., M.B.,<a href="https://catalogue.nli.ie/Record/vtls000638673">Mrs. Teresa Begge </a> , and Mrs Mary McCann. The Dictionary of Irish Biography contains information on a vast number of significant Irish figures, detailing their lives and interests. A few more of the Hope Collection’s sponsors are included in their records. <br><br>
<a href="http://www.dib.ie/biography/cox-michael-francis-a2127">The Rt. Hon. Michael Francis Cox, M.D.  </a> (1852-1926) was from Co. Roscommon, and had qualifications from the Catholic University School of Medicine Dublin, London University, Royal College of Surgeons Ireland, and the King and Queen's College of Physicians Ireland, both in the Arts and in medicine. He quickly entered the medical world and, despite being younger than many colleagues, became an incredibly respected physician. He worked with the Gaelic League to establish education on TB, and was appointed privy councillor in 1911, but resigned in 1920 to protest British policy in Ireland.  This did not halt his career, however, and he was made president of the Royal College of Physicians in Ireland (1922-24), and governor of the Catholic University Medical School and of the Royal Veterinary College of Ireland. Besides his medical career, Cox also participated in the Literary and Historical Society in his university, and won several medals and served many different roles. In 1908 he was offered the presidency of UCD’s governing body and declined, but later was appointed a member of this body. He was associated with several leaders of the nationalist movement, including Redmond, Parnell, Dillon and Davitt. Cox was a keen participant in Irish culture and language, and having bought the 17th century Book of Genealogies by Dubhaltach Mac Firbhisigh, he donated it to the UCD, with the intention of it being gained as a national possession. <br><br>
<a href=”https://www.dib.ie/biography/mcardle-john-stephen-a5549">Surgeon John Stephen McArdle </a> (1859-1928) was from Co. Louth, and received medical qualifications from the Catholic University Dublin, Royal College of Surgeons Ireland, Royal College of Physicians Ireland, Royal University of Ireland, and worked in St Vincent's Hospital Dublin. He was responsible for bringing innovative techniques to Ireland’s  surgical world. In 1904 he became a professor of surgery in the Catholic University and held the presidency of the Irish Medical Association from 1906 to 1908.  McArdle was well-known and liked across Dublin, especially for his work among the poor. UCD once held a life-sized portrait of him but this has since been lost. <br><br>
<a href="https://www.dib.ie/biography/martyn-edward-a5492">Edward Martyn</a> , Esq. (1859-1923) was from Co. Galway, with education from Belvedere College Dublin, and Beaumont College Windsor. Martyn was an Oxford drop out, a lover of the arts and of drama, a nationalist and a fan of Catholic ritual and Classical Civilisation. He rebuilt much of Galway’s Tulira Castle, and donated works by several legendary artists to the National Gallery of Ireland and donated great amounts of money to establish a Palestrina choir in Dublin - the Schola Cantorum - in 1903. He was also a major player in funding the Theatre of Ireland (1906) and the Irish Theatre (1914). The dramatist was president of a group called Na hAisteoirí, which was associated with the Gaelic League - which Martyn was also an executive member of.<br><br>
<a href="https://www.dib.ie/biography/dolan-joseph-a2664">Joseph Dolan</a> , Esq., M.A. (1872-1930) was born in Co. Louth, educated in St Stanislaus' School Dundalk, and the Jesuit colleges at Tullabeg and Clongowes, Royal University of Ireland, gaining both a BA and MA in Classical Studies. He then returned to Ardee to run his family business. Later he co-founded the Louth Historical and Archaeological Society, and was a member of the Royal Irish Academy. He was the secretary of the Ardee branch of the Gaelic League, and co-founded Irish-language summer schools in Omeath and Clogherhead. He helped Padraic Pearse found St Enda's by acting as financial guarantor in 1908. He was deeply involved in a variety of businesses in his hometown, was well-known for his charity work, and successfully lobbied for government housing for the people of Ardee.<br><br>
- Erin O'Reilly</p>

<h3>What Have We Got?</h3>
<h4>South Italian Artefacts</h4>
<p>The Southern Italian vases in Hope’s collection are primarily Campanian and Gnathian. The Campanian style is distinguished by its lighter coloured clay and, like all Southern Italian styles, by its dull black slipped surface. Common shapes included smaller types, such as oenochoai, as well as larger vessels, such as kraters. As seen on the Campanian vases in Hope’s collection, subject matter included distinct depictions of the local population, as well as mythological scenes. Gnathian is a subcategory of Apulian vase painting. The most common shapes were skyphoi, kantharoi, lekythoi, oenochoai, pelikes and bell-kraters. As demonstrated by the Gnathian ware in Hope’s collection, in the late 4th century BCE, the prominent imagery included floral motifs such as vines, ivy, and laurel. <br><br>
Southern Italian vase painting not only demonstrates the local development of style but also illustrates the life, customs, and interests of both Greek settlers and native populations during the time in which they were produced. <br><br>
<b>Hydriai</b><br><br>
Decorated hydriai, or water jars, were used to pour water into a krater to dilute wine during special occasions, such as symposia, or male drinking parties. These vessels have three handles: two on either side for carrying, and one on the back for pouring. While hydriai were often interacted with in male-dominated contexts, they were also used in female domestic contexts. One of the duties of women in classical antiquity was to fill hydriai with water at fountain houses and carry it back to the oikos (house). This activity often appears on painted hydriai. Other scenes of women in domestic contexts are depicted on hydriai, such as women weaving or sitting in the gynaeceum, the women’s quarters of the house. <br><br>
On the Attic hydria (UCD 190) from Hope’s collection, the lady of the house is seated in the gynaeceum surrounded by three female attendants. They hold an assortment of items, such as sashes, a mirror, a cosmetic box, and a kalathos, a basket associated with weaving. This scene represents female productivity, femininity, and beauty. To further illustrate this, above the women are three inscriptions of the word “ΚΑΛΗ,” the Greek feminine adjective of ‘good’, ‘beautiful’, or ‘lovely’.<br><br>
The Lucanian hydria (NMI 1917.41) in Hope’s collection has two characteristics of the Lucanian style: the woman’s wavy skirt and the man’s and woman’s heavy heads. The clay is a lighter shade, and the black is duller than Attic pottery. Following the Attic Classical style, the couple are depicted as young and beautiful, and, along with Eros, represent marital love. This is another example of a domestic scene. Rather than symbolising domestic female productivity, this hydria represents a connection to the oikos through the marital bond. <br><br>
- Rhea Bates
</p>
<h4>Greek Artefacts</h4>
<p>The items from Hope’s collection of pottery from the Eastern Mediterranean contains mostly Attic pieces, two plates from Rhodes, and one possibly Cycladic or Eastern Greek cup. These pieces span from the 7th century BCE, in the case of the Eastern Greek and Rhodian items, to the 4th century BCE in the case of the Attic piece depicting the Panathenaic Procession in Case 7.  <br><br>
The Cycladic islands are composed of the island chains in the Aegean Sea south of mainland Greece. Some of these islands include Naxos, Mykonos, Santorini, and Syros. These islands tend to have distinct artistic styles from mainland Greece, especially in pre-classical time periods. Rhodes is a Greek island in the far eastern Mediterranean near the coast of modern day Türkiye to the northeast of Crete. Due to many pieces of Eastern Greek pottery being found on Rhodes in the 19th century before different regional styles were classified, many pieces found on Rhodes have been later identified as likely coming from elsewhere. It is uncertain if this could be the case with these pieces. <br><br>
Rhodian pottery developed from the Middle Geometric period and is similar to other forms of Eastern Greek and Cycladic pottery. These pieces would have been made in the orientalising period of Eastern Greek pottery, which began around 680 BCE. It was during this period that artists began depicting animals on pottery, mainly vases. <br><br>
The Rhodian fruit dishes, as well as the Cycladic cup, have a clearly unique colour compared to Attic black and red figure pottery. The Rhodian dishes have a tan, beige base with black and red accents. One of the fruit dishes features “ray ornaments”, an adorning arrow, and sunray-like features across the inside of the plate. The balance of the plate itself appears to be warped, with one edge being higher than the other, likely reflecting uneven drying or firing. The other Rhodian dish features large black rings, intersected by brief sections of beige, and overlaid by relatively red rings within the black. The outermost and innermost beige rings feature black dots, while the middle of the three is empty; on display in the inner circle is a flower-like shape with black petals and red centre.  <br><br>
The cup, likely an earlier piece and potentially from the Cycladic islands, features more red clay colour than the Rhodian plates. There are traces of black on one side, and it is adorned with beige as well, but most of the base and markings are red clay. This separates it from the two Rhodian dishes.  <br><br>
- Cooper Hays
</p>
